Expert Q&A

Frequently Asked Questions about farming, answered by our experts.

Improving soil health organically is key. Focus on:
  • Composting: Use farm waste, manure, and kitchen scraps to add rich organic matter.
  • Cover Crops: Plant legumes (clover, vetch) or grasses (rye) in the off-season.
  • Crop Rotation: Avoid planting the same crop in the same spot every year.
  • Mulching: Apply straw or wood chips to conserve moisture and add organic matter.
Organic pest control focuses on prevention and natural predators.
  • Introduce Beneficial Insects: Ladybugs, lacewings, and parasitic wasps prey on pests.
  • Neem Oil: A natural pesticide that disrupts insect life cycles.
  • Companion Planting: Plant marigolds, garlic, or basil to repel pests.
  • Floating Row Covers: A physical barrier to prevent insects from landing on plants.
Drip irrigation is a method that saves water by allowing it to drip slowly and directly to the roots. It's efficient because it:
  • Reduces evaporation by applying water directly to the soil.
  • Prevents weed growth by only watering the crops.
  • Saves time as the system can be automated.
  • Delivers nutrients directly to the roots (a process called "fertigation").
Crop rotation is planting different types of crops in the same area in sequential seasons. It's vital for:
  • Nutrient Management: Legumes (beans, peas) add nitrogen to the soil, which feeds "heavy feeders" like corn.
  • Pest and Disease Control: By removing a pest's food source for a season, you break its life cycle.
  • Improved Soil Structure: Different root systems (deep vs. shallow) break up the soil at different levels.
Companion planting is growing different plants near each other for a mutual benefit. For example:
  • Pest Repulsion: Planting marigolds near tomatoes can repel nematodes and tomato hornworms.
  • Attracting Pollinators: Planting borage or lavender can attract bees, which helps pollinate nearby fruit and vegetable crops.
  • The "Three Sisters": A classic example. Corn provides a trellis for beans, beans fix nitrogen for the corn, and squash provides ground cover to keep weeds down.
You can test your soil pH in two main ways:
  • Digital/Probe Tester: These are available at garden centers. You simply insert the metal probe into moist soil and get an instant reading.
  • DIY Kit (Litmus Paper): These kits require you to mix a soil sample with distilled water, then dip a test strip into the solution and compare the color to a chart.
  • Lab Testing: For the most accurate results, you can send a soil sample to your local agricultural extension office or a private lab. They will give you a detailed report on pH and nutrient levels.

Most vegetables prefer a slightly acidic to neutral pH (around 6.0 to 7.0).

  • Non-GMO (Genetically Modified Organism): This means the plant's DNA was not altered in a lab. A product can be Non-GMO but still grown using chemical pesticides and herbicides.
  • Certified Organic: This is a much stricter standard. To be certified organic, a product must *also* be Non-GMO, *and* it must be grown without the use of most synthetic chemical pesticides, herbicides, or fertilizers.

In short: All organic food is Non-GMO, but not all Non-GMO food is organic.

Both are excellent soil conditioners, but they are different:
  • Manure: This is animal waste (from cows, chickens, horses, etc.). "Fresh" manure is very high in nitrogen and can "burn" plants. It should be "aged" or composted for at least 6 months before use.
  • Compost: This is the result of decomposing mixed organic matter, which can include manure, but also kitchen scraps (vegetable peels), yard waste (leaves, grass clippings), and paper. Well-made compost is a balanced soil amendment that is ready to use immediately.
There is no single answer, as it depends on your **soil type, weather, and crop type**. The best rule is:
  • Check the Soil: Don't water on a fixed schedule. Instead, stick your finger 2-3 inches into the soil near the plant. If it feels dry at that depth, it's time to water.
  • Water Deeply, Less Often: It's better to give your plants a deep, thorough watering (so the moisture soaks down to the deep roots) every few days, rather than a light sprinkle every day.
  • Water in the Morning: Watering in the early morning is most efficient, as less water is lost to evaporation and the leaves have time to dry before nightfall, which prevents fungal diseases.
Plants will often tell you what they need. Look for these common signs:
  • Yellowing older leaves (bottom of plant): This is a classic sign of **Nitrogen (N)** deficiency.
  • Yellowing *new* leaves (top of plant): This often points to an **Iron (Fe)** deficiency.
  • Purple or reddish tint on leaves: This is a common sign of **Phosphorus (P)** deficiency, especially in young plants.
  • Yellowing or brown edges on leaves: This often indicates a **Potassium (K)** deficiency.
    • When you see these signs, applying a balanced compost or an appropriate organic fertilizer can help correct the issue.
Integrated Pest Management (IPM) is a smarter, more sustainable approach to controlling pests. Instead of just spraying pesticides, it involves multiple steps:
  • 1. Prevention: Choosing disease-resistant crop varieties and practicing good sanitation (like removing dead plants) to prevent pests from showing up.
  • 2. Monitoring: Regularly scouting your fields to identify pests and see if their numbers are high enough to cause real damage.
  • 3. Biological Controls: Using natural predators (like ladybugs) or companion planting *before* using chemicals.
  • 4. Chemical Controls (As a Last Resort): If all other methods fail, using the most targeted and least toxic pesticide possible to handle the problem.

IPM saves money, reduces environmental pollution, and is more effective in the long run.
